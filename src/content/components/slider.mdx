## How it works

- The `<input>` is a 16px-tall transparent box (`h-4 bg-transparent`) — just big enough to hold
  the thumb — and the _visible_ track is the runnable-track pseudo (`::-webkit-slider-runnable-track`
  / `::-moz-range-track`, both `h-1.5 bg-secondary rounded-full`). The thumb is styled twice,
  `[&::-webkit-slider-thumb]:` and `[&::-moz-range-thumb]:`, and each engine ignores the other's
  selectors because every utility compiles to its own rule.
- On WebKit the 16px thumb rides a 6px track, so `[&::-webkit-slider-thumb]:-mt-[5px]` drops it
  back onto center ((16 − 6) / 2). Firefox lines `::-moz-range-thumb` up with `::-moz-range-track`
  by itself.
- The fill is the fun part. Firefox just hands it to you: `[&::-moz-range-progress]:bg-primary`
  and the left side is filled. WebKit still has no filled-track pseudo-element — so we borrow a
  timeline. The thumb exposes a `view-timeline`, the input reads it through `timeline-scope`, and
  an `animation` drives a registered `--range-fill` property that a `linear-gradient` paints onto
  the track. No JS, no `oninput` — the fill is a pure function of where the thumb sits. (That part
  lives in a separate CSS block: `@property` and `view-timeline` have no utility form.)
- Where scroll-driven animations haven't landed yet, `@supports` skips the whole block and you're
  left with a plain secondary track — still perfectly usable. So the old "WebKit can't fill a
  range without JS" line was only ever half true: not impossible, just not everywhere _yet_.
